What Your Results Mean

Your unfair dismissal compensation has two distinct components, each calculated differently. The basic award is a fixed statutory sum based on a formula identical to redundancy pay. The compensatory award depends on your actual financial losses as assessed by the tribunal, making it harder to predict precisely (Employment Rights Act 1996, Part X).

Your Basic Award

The basic award is a fixed statutory amount calculated using the same formula as redundancy pay. This is your minimum entitlement and cannot be reduced except in specific circumstances such as contributory fault. The maximum basic award for 2025/26 is £21,570, based on 20 years at 1.5 weeks at the £719 weekly cap.

Your Compensatory Award

The compensatory award covers your actual financial losses: lost earnings, pension contributions, benefits, and expenses. Tribunals assess this individually based on evidence you provide. The statutory cap for 2025/26 is £118,223 or 52 weeks’ gross pay, whichever is lower.

Important: Compensation can be reduced if the tribunal finds you contributed to your dismissal (contributory fault), or if you have not taken reasonable steps to find new work (failure to mitigate losses). The Acas Code uplift can increase or decrease awards by up to 25% depending on whether correct procedures were followed.

The median unfair dismissal tribunal award was £13,541 in 2023/24, with the maximum compensatory award capped at £118,223 for 2025/26. However, actual payouts vary widely: the top 10% of awards exceeded £40,000 while the bottom quartile received under £5,000 (Ministry of Justice, Tribunal Statistics Quarterly, 2024).

How the Calculation Works

This calculator uses the statutory formula set out in the Employment Rights Act 1996 (as amended), with rates effective from 6 April 2025. The basic award formula is identical to statutory redundancy pay. The Secretary of State updates the weekly pay cap and compensatory award limit each April (ERA 1996, s.227).

Basic Award Formula

Age at Dismissal Multiplier per Year of Service
Under 22 0.5 weeks’ pay per year
22 to 40 1 week’s pay per year
41 and over 1.5 weeks’ pay per year

Weekly pay is capped at £719 regardless of actual earnings. Years of service are capped at 20 years. Maximum possible basic award: 20 × 1.5 × £719 = £21,570.

Compensatory Award

Based on actual financial losses proven to the tribunal. Includes: loss of net earnings (net pay × weeks unemployed), loss of statutory rights (conventionally £500), pension loss (if applicable), and expenses incurred. Capped at £118,223 or 52 weeks’ gross pay, whichever is lower (2025/26).

Benefits Repayment

If you received benefits (JSA, Universal Credit) during the period covered by your compensatory award, the Department for Work and Pensions may recoup these from your compensation under the Employment Protection (Recoupment of Benefits) Regulations 1996.

Source: Employment Rights Act 1996, updated annually by the Secretary of State. Current rates effective 6 April 2025. Next update expected April 2026.

The unfair dismissal basic award uses the same formula as statutory redundancy pay: 0.5 weeks’ pay per year of service under age 22, 1 week between ages 22 and 40, and 1.5 weeks from age 41 onwards. Weekly pay is capped at £719 and service at 20 years, giving a maximum basic award of £21,570 for 2025/26 (ERA 1996, s.119).

Settlement vs. Tribunal: Which Is Right for You?

Most unfair dismissal cases settle before reaching a full tribunal hearing. Around 50% of employment tribunal claims are resolved through Acas early conciliation or private settlement, according to Ministry of Justice statistics (2024). The right choice depends on your financial situation, the strength of your evidence, and how quickly you need resolution.

If your calculated compensation falls within the range your employer is offering as a settlement, accepting may provide faster, more certain financial resolution. Settlement agreements are legally binding and typically include a reference clause, which can help your future job search.

However, if your employer has acted particularly badly — for example, dismissing you for whistleblowing or asserting a statutory right — compensation is uncapped at tribunal. In these cases, going to tribunal may be the better financial decision.

Roughly half of all unfair dismissal claims are settled before reaching a full tribunal hearing, typically through Acas early conciliation. Settlement agreements offer faster, more certain outcomes (1–4 weeks versus 6–12 months for tribunal), but tribunal awards can be higher when evidence is strong (Ministry of Justice, 2024).

Frequently Asked Questions About Unfair Dismissal Compensation

What is the average settlement for unfair dismissal?

The median award for unfair dismissal at tribunal was £13,541 in 2023/24 (Ministry of Justice Employment Tribunal Statistics). However, most cases settle before reaching tribunal. Settlement amounts typically range from 1 to 6 months’ gross salary, depending on the strength of your case, your length of service, and the employer’s willingness to negotiate. For employees earning £35,000, this means a settlement range of roughly £2,900 to £17,500.

How much do you get for unfair dismissal if you win?

If you win at tribunal, you receive a basic award (calculated using the same formula as redundancy pay, maximum £21,570 in 2025/26) plus a compensatory award for actual financial losses (capped at £118,223 or 52 weeks’ gross pay, whichever is lower). The basic award is guaranteed; the compensatory award depends on your proven losses. Use the calculator above to estimate your basic award based on your specific age, pay, and service.

Is it better to settle or go to tribunal?

It depends on your circumstances. Settlements offer faster, more certain outcomes — typically resolved in 1 to 4 weeks compared to 6 to 12 months for tribunal. Around 50% of all claims settle through Acas conciliation. If your calculated compensation is within the range of your employer’s settlement offer, accepting may give you faster financial resolution. However, tribunal is worth considering if your employer has acted particularly badly or you have very strong evidence, as awards can exceed settlement offers significantly.

How often do unfair dismissal claims win?

Approximately 5–10% of unfair dismissal claims that reach a full tribunal hearing are successful (Ministry of Justice, 2024). This sounds low, but the number is misleading. Around 50% of all claims are resolved through settlement before reaching a hearing. Many “withdrawn” cases reflect private settlements. The overall resolution rate — including settlements — is significantly higher than the hearing success rate alone.

Is it worth getting a solicitor for unfair dismissal?

It depends on the complexity and value of your case. For straightforward claims with smaller amounts, Citizens Advice provides free guidance and can help you prepare. For complex cases or claims exceeding £10,000, many employment solicitors offer free initial consultations and no-win-no-fee arrangements. Solicitor costs typically range from £750 to £5,000 for full tribunal representation. Weigh the potential compensation against legal costs before deciding.
